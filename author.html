Arundhati Roy

Arundhati Roy (born 24 November 1961) is an Indian author and political activist best known for her novel The God of Small Things (1997), which won the Booker Prize for Fiction in 1997 and became an international bestseller. She is also a prominent social critic and has been involved in various environmental, human rights, and anti-globalization causes.

Roy's writing combines lyrical prose with sharp political commentary, making her one of the most distinctive voices in contemporary literature. Beyond fiction, she has written extensively on issues including nuclear weapons, environmental degradation, and social justice, often challenging mainstream narratives and government policies in India.

Early Life and Education

Roy was born in Shillong, Meghalaya, to a Bengali Hindu father and a Syrian Christian mother, Mary Roy. Her childhood was marked by the separation of her parents when she was young, and her subsequent upbringing in Aymanam, Kerala, by her mother, who was herself a noted social activist and educator who challenged inheritance laws in India.

She studied architecture at the School of Planning and Architecture in Delhi, where she met architect Gerard da Cunha, whom she later married (the marriage ended in divorce). Although she never practiced architecture professionally, her training influenced her keen sense of structure and space, which is evident in her literary work. During this time, she lived in a squatters' colony and supported herself by selling beer bottles.

Literary Career

The God of Small Things

Published in 1997, The God of Small Things became an international bestseller and established Roy as a major literary figure. The semi-autobiographical novel tells the story of fraternal twins Rahel and Estha in Kerala, and explores themes of forbidden love, caste discrimination, childhood trauma, and the destruction caused by the "Love Laws" that dictate "who should be loved, and how. And how much."

The book's non-linear narrative structure and poetic language captivated readers worldwide. Roy's innovative use of language includes breaking conventional grammar rules, creating new compound words, and employing distinctive capitalization patterns to emphasize certain concepts.

The novel won the Booker Prize in 1997, making Roy the first Indian woman and the first non-expatriate Indian to receive the award. It has since been translated into more than 40 languages and has sold millions of copies globally. The book received widespread critical acclaim, though some criticized it for its explicit content and portrayal of Syrian Christian society in Kerala.

Return to Fiction

After a 20-year hiatus from fiction during which she focused on political activism and essay writing, Roy published The Ministry of Utmost Happiness in 2017. The novel weaves together multiple narratives set against the backdrop of contemporary Indian politics and social movements, including the Kashmir conflict and the displacement of tribal peoples.

The novel features a diverse cast of characters, including a transgender woman named Anjum who lives in a graveyard in Old Delhi. While the book received mixed reviews, many praised Roy's ambitious scope and her commitment to giving voice to marginalized communities in India.

Political Activism and Essays

Following the success of The God of Small Things, Roy turned her attention to political activism and essay writing. She has been a vocal critic of various aspects of Indian politics, economic policies, and social structures.

Her essay "The End of Imagination" (1998) was a powerful critique of India's nuclear weapons tests. Her collection The Cost of Living (1999) criticized both the nuclear program and the construction of large dams, particularly the Narmada Dam project, which displaced hundreds of thousands of people. She has consistently advocated for the rights of tribal communities (Adivasis) displaced by development projects.

Her political writing addresses a wide range of issues:

• Environmental activism and opposition to large dam projects
• Critique of neoliberal economic policies and globalization
• Opposition to nuclear weapons programs in South Asia
• Support for Kashmiri self-determination
• Criticism of Hindu nationalism and communalism
• Advocacy for marginalized communities including Dalits and Adivasis
• Opposition to American foreign policy and imperialism

Her outspoken positions have been controversial and have led to legal challenges. In 2002, she was sentenced to one day of symbolic imprisonment and fined for contempt of court related to her criticism of the Indian Supreme Court's handling of the Narmada Dam case. She has faced numerous other legal cases and threats related to her activism.

Roy's major essay collections include The Algebra of Infinite Justice (2002), An Ordinary Person's Guide to Empire (2004), Listening to Grasshoppers (2009), Walking with the Comrades (2011), and Capitalism: A Ghost Story (2014).

Writing Style and Themes

Roy's literary style is characterized by several distinctive features. In her fiction, she employs lyrical, poetic prose with innovative use of language, often breaking conventional grammatical rules to create emphasis or meaning. Her narratives frequently use non-linear structures, moving back and forth through time.

Thematically, Roy's work consistently explores social hierarchies, particularly India's caste system and its devastating effects on individuals and communities. She examines power dynamics and gives voice to the marginalized and oppressed. Her writing blends the personal and political, showing how large-scale political events affect individual lives.

In her political essays, Roy's style is direct, passionate, and often polemical. She uses powerful imagery and metaphors to make complex political and economic issues accessible to general readers. Her essays combine detailed factual reporting with moral urgency and literary craft.

Her work often challenges conventional boundaries—whether linguistic, social, or political—and insists on the interconnection between art and politics. She has been critical of the notion that artists should remain apolitical, arguing that silence in the face of injustice is itself a political act.

Awards and Recognition

Roy has received numerous awards for both her literary work and her activism:

• 1997: Man Booker Prize for The God of Small Things
• 2002: Lannan Foundation Cultural Freedom Prize
• 2004: Sydney Peace Prize for her work in social campaigns and advocacy of non-violence
• 2006: Sahitya Akademi Award (which she declined in solidarity with other writers)
• 2011: Norman Mailer Prize for Distinguished Writing
• 2019: NYCLU Callaway Prize for defending liberty

Despite these honors, Roy has often been a controversial figure in India, with some accusing her of being anti-national, while others celebrate her as a fearless voice for justice.

Legacy and Influence

Roy remains one of the most influential contemporary Indian writers, known equally for her literary achievements and her political activism. She has demonstrated that it is possible to be both a serious artist and a committed activist, refusing to separate these roles.

She has inspired a generation of writers from the Global South to engage with social and political issues while maintaining artistic integrity. Her success opened doors for other Indian writers writing in English and challenged Western publishing's narrow focus on certain types of postcolonial narratives.

Her activism has brought international attention to issues affecting marginalized communities in India that might otherwise remain invisible. Her willingness to face legal consequences for her beliefs has made her a symbol of intellectual courage and resistance.

Critics of Roy argue that her political positions are sometimes oversimplified or that she romanticizes certain movements. However, her supporters contend that she performs the essential function of speaking truth to power and amplifying voices that are typically silenced.
